BuildingConnected

Overview

From May to August 2018, I worked as a Product Design Intern at BuildingConnected, an Autodesk company in San Francisco, CA. BuildingConnected helps construction professionals bid for projects, meet deadlines, and network with other professionals in the same field. Over $75 billion in new construction is managed on BuildingConnected every month.

During my internship, I was the designer on one large project: improving the calendar for subcontractors. The new calendar was shipped to almost a quarter million users and has increased sales.

Problem

The calendar currently does not help subcontractors plan ahead for upcoming work, visualize events in groups, make quick updates to events, or find relevant information efficiently.

Solution

A calendar that displays in week and month views, and shows grouped events together. Users can click on an event to bring up a pop-up card that shows more information that can be edited. Overall, the calendar’s visual design quickly informs the user of information about the event.

Work overview

  1. Validation research for the new calendar product
  2. Ideation and low-fidelity wireframes of every feature
  3. Flesh out designs for MVP features (ideation, wireframing, prototyping, usability testing)
  4. Help engineers implement designs, and test dogfood code

Problems and goals

Before I started at BC, the team had completed discovery research, as well as early validation research. The problem brief handed off to me outlined the problems and suggested solutions below:

  • Problem #1: Agenda calendar provides a limited view and does not help users plan ahead for upcoming work
  • Solution: Week and month views
  • Problem #2: Calendar does not surface all the relevant info to subcontractors
  • Solution: Pop-up card (displays when user clicks on an event)
  • Problem #3: Calendar often displays information that's not relevant to the action a user is trying to accomplish
  • Solution: Better filters and general scannability in visual design
  • Problem #4: Related opportunities create multiple calendar events
  • Solution: Grouping events
  • Problem #5: Users cannot take actions or make quick updates to their events
  • Solution: Pop-up card

Validation research

Working closely with a PM, I conducted research to continue validating the problems and solution direction. I wrote interview scripts, created wireframes, talked to BC users via video chat, and summarized results.

Our main research objectives were:

  1. Explore how users think of due dates for different event types (Bids, Job Walks, RFIs) in grouped opportunities
  2. Confirm user needs for the functionality and content of Agenda, Week, and Month views
  3. Confirm user needs for the content of pop-up card
  4. Determine the extent to which users would like to take action / make changes in the calendar

Below is a low-fidelity wireframe I created to help interviewees visualize concepts.

Our research revealed widely varying user needs. For example, some users were happy using Agenda view, while some wanted a Month view displaying 5 or 6 weeks. Initially, I was confused about the research results, since they didn’t seem to reveal any specific patterns. While I would have liked to take the time to interview more users of specific types, I didn’t have this luxury in a fast-paced startup. BC is used by many types of companies with users of many types of roles, and thus constructing representative personas would have been costly. However, with the research we did have time for, I consolidated the main differences between two different user types and their use cases for BC calendar:

Manager

  • Make sure the team has enough work for the upcoming weeks
  • Make sure team members are submitting Bids on time
  • Go over Undecided bid invitations and accept/decline them
  • Assign upcoming “Bids Due” events to team members

Estimator

  • See what opportunities/events are assigned to him/her
  • Meet deadlines and stay on top of work: submit Bids and RFIs on time, and go to scheduled Job Walks

Overall, I realized that I simply had to design for flexibility, both in terms of satisfying user needs and building on the product in the future.

Exploring the ideal calendar

At this point in my internship, half of my time was devoted to conducting validation research. The other half of my time was spent using research results to explore concepts of the ideal calendar. These low-fidelity wireframes helped me estimate the time required to flesh out designs.

I also presented these designs to engineers and PMs, and together we determined what features would be included in the MVP, or first release, of BC Calendar to be completed by the end of the third quarter.

Agenda, Week, and Month views

We decided that the main MVP feature to be built for Calendar was the different views. Agenda view would be revamped, and Week and Month views would be new features.

User needs

As always, I started with user needs. While some of the user needs for Agenda, Week and Month views were gleaned from other calendar apps (especially GCal and Outlook, which was popular among BC users), we also found BC-specific user needs from our validation research.

Some key user needs were:

  • Hiding weekends by default, since subcontractors had no events on weekends. This would allow for more horizontal screen real estate.
  • Displaying events as instances, rather than a period of time. All types of events in BC (Bids, Job Walks, RFIs) are basically types of due dates.
  • For month view: a rolling calendar. Users disliked how other calendar apps displayed one month per page. For the MVP, we decided to have a paginated month Calendar, with 4 weeks displayed per page. The upcoming 4 weeks would be displayed by default (on the first page).
  • For week view: listing events with the same date/time vertically, since subcontractors have many events at the same time. Listing events horizontally would cut off the name of the event far too soon, rendering them unreadable.
  • Displaying the most important information in event blocks: the date/time of the event, assignee, opportunity name, client name, event type, status, notes, and trade.

An example of a problem our users ran into while using typical calendar apps. In the pre-construction industry, events (like Bids Due) are usually due at a standard time, like 12 pm. As a result, calendars that listed events horizontally were unable to display useful information; the text would be truncated.

Ideation and rough sketches

Keeping in mind consistent design throughout Calendar, I ideated on layout and functionality for Agenda, Week, and Month views by sketching out my ideas.

Iterating in Sketch

In Sketch, creating mockups of the Calendar views' structure and functionality was simple. I mostly iterated on the visual design of the event blocks across all views. The biggest challenge lay in presenting many bits of event information, yet also maintaining simplicity, clarity, and scannability.

I found presenting the event’s status and type the most challenging. I tried displaying both with icons, but found this hard to scan; I tried conveying status with patterns and type with color, but found this visually noisy and overwhelming. Event status and type were also the hardest pieces of information to rank by importance. A manager might think status is more important, while an estimator might think type is more important. In the end, I decided to prioritize event status over type, since a manager, who would most likely be looking at all events at once, would care more about an event’s status.

Prototyping in Invision and usability testing

To ensure that my designs made sense and met user needs, I set up unmoderated usability tests with a link to my Invision prototype and recruited BC users, as well as random users. After analyzing the results, I realized that I needed to tweak the colors of the event blocks so that they were easily visible to all users, as well as add tooltips to the icons indicating event type. After one more round of usability testing, I knew that I had reached my final designs.

Supporting Grouping in Agenda: pop-up card

The most urgent update needed for the MVP, or the other feature I designed, was displaying Grouped events together in Calendar. Because the Grouping functionality was a new feature just released elsewhere in the product, we needed to also support Grouping in Calendar. This would create consistency throughout the product, as well as de-clutter the Calendar.

To support Grouping in Calendar, I needed to design the pop-up card that would appear when a user clicks on an event. While previously iterating on the ideal calendar, I had determined that users should view an event’s additional information, including Group information, in the pop-up card.

Besides designing a pop-up card, I also made minimal visual design improvements to Agenda, mostly focusing on spacing and organization of content.

User needs

To design the pop-up card, I started with user needs. From my previous validation research, I had determined the content for pop-up card, and the extent to which users would like to take action / make changes in the calendar (research objectives #3 and #4).

One key decision I had to make was if/how I should display the Priority field in Calendar. Through user research, I found that user needs and current behavior varied widely. Some users never filled out the Priority field for opportunities, while others insisted on color-coding opportunities and events by Priority. To accommodate these different needs for the MVP, I decided to not display Priority on the event block, but display it in the pop-up card and also allow users to edit the field from the pop-up card. This would make Priority easily viewable and editable if needed, but not create more visual noise than necessary for the time being.

Lessons learned

I definitely learned a lot during my summer at BC. Here are some of my top lessons learned:

  • Effectively utilizing user research results to make design decisions.
  • Technical design skills, especially in visual design (working with spacing, fonts, hierarchy, color, etc).
  • Presenting design work to garner actionable feedback from execs and other designers, as well as providing helpful feedback.
  • Communicating clearly and often with PMs and engineers, especially in discussing tradeoffs and production plans.
  • How startups set and accomplish goals, and generally how they function.
