Swift Exchange

Overview

At the start of every semester, college students must buy textbooks for their classes, and many opt to buy used books for the lower prices. Duke students currently use a Facebook group, Duke Textbook Exchange, to buy and sell used books. As a user of this group myself, I noticed many problems and inconveniences with using this group. Thus, I decided to challenge myself by designing a website for Duke students to efficiently buy and sell used textbooks.

Problem

Duke students have no means to efficiently buy and sell used textbooks. Using the Duke Textbook Exchange Facebook group isn't an ideal experience.

Solution

A website that makes the process of buying and selling used books more efficient by 1) helping sellers fill in detailed information for their listings, 2) expediting the buyer’s process of finding his/her desired book, and 3) helping the buyer and seller easily meet up to make the exchange.

Problem

  • Textbooks are expensive.
  • Students currently buy and sell used textbooks in a Duke Facebook group, which has several problems for this use case. The group is inefficient, there is no consistency with information posted on listings, and users don't mark items as sold.

A screenshot showing example posts in the Duke Textbook Exchange group.

Solution overview

  1. Sellers post a listing for a book they want to sell on the website. Sellers are encouraged or required to fill in detailed information about their used books.
  2. Buyers browse book listings posted by sellers.
  3. If a buyer wants to buy a book and reserves it, the buyer and seller can view each other’s contact information.
  4. The buyer and seller meet up on campus to make the exchange.
  5. The listing for the bought book is removed from the catalogue.

A storyboard of the users' journey.

Product goals

I defined general goals to act as touchstones throughout the project:

  • The website should be intuitive, efficient, and trustworthy.
  • Duke students will use this website instead of the Facebook group to buy/sell textbooks.

Design process outline

  1. Identify user needs via research and interviews
  2. Define overall user flow
  3. Ideate and sketch screens on paper
  4. Low-fidelity wireframes in Sketch
  5. High-fidelity wireframes in Sketch and prototyping in Invision

User needs

I identified/validated user needs by interviewing students. My research objectives were:

  1. Identify students' userflows with using the Facebook group, and their painpoints.
  2. Identify features that the Facebook group lacks.
  3. Find out why people don’t use the Facebook group.
  4. Ask exploratory, broad questions to determine overall user needs of buyers and sellers, keeping in mind that students are often both. How do I design a product that benefits buyers and sellers equally?

With research objective #4, a point I had to keep in mind was that buyers want lower prices, while sellers want higher prices — how would I make the product “fair” for both buyers and sellers, who are often the same person? Through user research, I found that even though sellers might want to make a profit, most are more concerned with selling as quickly as possible, at lower prices if necessary. Furthermore, sometimes buyers would buy books for higher prices, if that meant they could obtain the item quickly. In other words, buyers and sellers share the common user need of efficiency.

Thus, I decided to prioritize designing for efficiency for users.

One key decision was whether or not I should allow buyers and sellers to negotiate prices. Since I usually negotiate, I was surprised to find through user research that most people do not negotiate, since they want to buy/sell quickly. Thus, I decided to not allow users to negotiate; this would increase efficiency.

User needs of a buyer

Since I wanted to design the userflow of a buyer first, I analyzed my interview results to conslidate the user needs of a buyer:

  • Find specific books quickly and easily, by browsing detailed and accurate listings
  • Find the cheapest books
  • Obtain books quickly

Defining userflow of a buyer

After conducting user research, I sketched out a flowchart of the user's journey. Most buyers use the Facebook group with the intent of finding a specific book, so they almost always start with the Search feature of Facebook groups. Thus, I decided that a buyer's user flow for my website should also begin with Search.

Ideation

With the user flowchart as a guide, I sketched out a broad range of rough wireframes for consideration.

Low-fidelity wireframes

Using Sketch, I refined my sketches through low-fidelity wireframes that were grayscale with approximate spacing. At this stage, I focused on nailing down overall page layouts and functionality. Explorations included whether to display search results in a grid or list layout, and whether to display specific listings in separate pages or a side panel. Throughout the iterative process, I interviewed users and conducted usability tests to ensure that my designs were on the right track.

High-fidelity wireframes

Once I had finished overall page layouts, the next step was defining a design system and more nuanced user interactions. Explorations included how to indicate the difference between action links and regular links, as well as using toasts after bookmarking listings.
